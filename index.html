Intro Math Courses at Brown

There are dozens of introductory level math and applied math courses at Brown. Figuring out which ones you're qualified for and deciding which one to take can be extremely frustrating. Hopefully this site can help you figure out what to take!

Keep in mind that you can take most courses without the formal prerequisites, as long as you get the permission of the instructor (just send them an email before reading period is over!).

There are some subtopics that these courses rely upon that you did not learn in your high school calculus course. This does not always mean you should not be in that course! Some things you can do in this situation are:

  • Email the professor asking how important the material is to learn
  • Learn the subtopic on your own, using a resource like Youtube, Khan Academy, or a textbook.
  • Ask a friend for help! A lot of Brown students have taken a few calc/linear algebra courses and are hopefully willing to help you through hard spots.

    Calculus III is a course on multivariable calculus, or the study of calculus in multiple dimensions. For example you will be finding volumes of three dimensional shapes or learning how flows move through different surfaces.

    MATH 0180 and MATH 0200 cover the same topics, but the latter is more suited to the needs of physics and engineering students. MATH 0350 is intended for students leaning towards pure mathematics and involves the teaching and testing of proofs of theorems. This class thus is more time-consuming and harder than its proof-devoid counterpart, MATH 0180. Recommended for students concentrating in mathematics (not applied mathematics).

    If you've taken a Calculus II course, you should be prepared for Calculus III. You can brush up on Calc I/II on this site

    Parts of Calc I/II to be most comfortable with are:

    • Limit definition of derivative.
    • Derivatives and integrals.
    • Anything that happened in three dimensions (for example, surfaces of revolution).
    • Vectors are taught at the beginning of these courses, but having an intuition for them off the bat can be helpful. Quick video 1 (Khan Academy), quick video 2 (3blue1brown, don't watch the next part unless you want to learn linear algebra), long video (Professor Leonard - great resource throughout Calc III)

    We recommend students only take MATH 540 if they are interested in pursuing a pure math concentration. If you are less interested in theory and proofs, it is not the class for you! MATH 520 provides an equally strong grounding in linear algebra, and will well-prepare you for future applications of the key concepts.

    It is also worth noting that, while MATH 540 is a larger lecture, and often includes a lot of proofs, MATH 520 has many more sections, some of which are taught by graduate students and thus capped at ~30 students. If you work better within smaller classrooms settings, this may be another reason to take MATH 520.

    Note that you do not need to know a lot of calculus to take linear algebra. The amount of calculus you are comfortable with depends on the professor, so you should ask and brush up on the needed topics.

    There are some topics you should be comfortable with before taking 520 or 540 though:

    • Vectors: Quick video 1 (Khan Academy), quick video 2 (3blue1brown)
    • Imaginary numbers. Brushup
    • Polynomials. Brushup
    • Be comfortable with lots of basic arithmetic and algebra because it comes up a lot.

    The Applied Mathematics concentration and its affiliates (e.g., APMA-CS, APMA-ECON) all strongly recommend students take the 350/360 track instead of the 330/340 track. The later is intended for students who need knowledge of ODEs/PDEs, but do not necessarily intend to explore the topic much deeper in the future. For example, many engineers take this sequence. While we strongly recommend intended APMA concentrators take the 350/360 sequence because later classes build on the subject matter, this may be less important for dual concentrators. For example, there are many high-level APMA-ECON classes that do not require these as a prerequisite.

    To take an ODE course, you should be comfortable with the following topics:

    • Derivatives. In particular, the chain rule is very useful in differential equations.
    • Integration. In particular, integration by substitution and by parts. You should be comfortable with most of the integrals discussed on this playlist.
    • Partial Fraction Decomposition. Short video (patrickJMT), Long video (organic chemistry tutor)
    • Some trig identities and basic trig integration.

    For a firm grounding in statistics, we recommend students take APMA 1650. While APMA 650 provides a cursory introduction of the subject, it is predominantly intended for non-concentrators or students who will not need to use much statistics later in their college career. If you intend to be any sort of APMA concentrator, 1650 is required for many upper-level classes, and cannot be substituted with 650.

    If you want an extra challenge, APMA 1655 includes more theory-based problems and is slightly more difficult than 1650. However, there is no difference in your preparedness level for other APMA classes down the line.
